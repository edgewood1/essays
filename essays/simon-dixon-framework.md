# Simon Dixon: How the World Actually Works
---

## Who Is Simon Dixon?

Simon Dixon is a former investment banker who left the industry in 2006 after becoming disillusioned with how the financial system operated from the inside. He went on to become an early Bitcoin investor and backer of major crypto companies including Coinbase, Kraken, and Bitfinex. Over the years he has developed a framework for understanding global power — not as a set of conspiracy theories, but as a map of incentives, institutions, and structures that most people are never taught to see.

His core argument: the world isn't chaotic or mismanaged. Dixon believes it is functioning exactly as designed — that the disorder, inequality, and recurring crises most people attribute to incompetence or bad luck are actually the predictable outputs of a system built to produce them. The people who appear to be in charge — presidents, prime ministers, central bankers — are, in his view, largely performing roles within a system they did not build and cannot fundamentally change. The real levers of power sit elsewhere.

Others push back on this framing. The counterargument is that what looks like design is more often the accumulated result of competing interests, historical accidents, and institutional inertia — that no one is steering, and that attributing coherent intent to a system this large gives it more credit than it deserves. The debate, at its core, is whether the world's problems reflect a plan or simply a pattern.

## The Three Industrial Complexes

Dixon organizes global power into three interlocking structures he calls "complexes." Each one feeds and enables the others. None operates in isolation.

### The Financial-Industrial Complex (FIC)

This is the foundation. Everything else depends on it.

At its core, the FIC controls who gets access to money and on what terms. To understand how, it helps to picture the layers from top down.

**At the top: the BIS (Bank for International Settlements)**
Most people have never heard of it. Based in Basel, Switzerland, the BIS is sometimes called "the central bank of central banks." It does not deal with ordinary people or businesses — it exists solely to coordinate policy between the world's central banks. When the heads of the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, and the Bank of England need to align on global monetary strategy, they do it through the BIS. It sets the rules that national banking systems operate under. There is no democratic oversight of it. It is essentially a private club for the most powerful financial institutions on earth.

**One level down: central banks**
A central bank is not like the bank you have a checking account at. Your bank — Chase, Wells Fargo, a local credit union — is a commercial bank. It takes deposits, makes loans, and earns profit. A central bank is something different: it is the bank that commercial banks themselves borrow from. It is also the institution that, in most countries, creates the national currency.

In the United States, that institution is the **Federal Reserve** (the "Fed"), created in 1913. The Fed sets the interest rate at which commercial banks can borrow money overnight. When the Fed raises rates, borrowing becomes more expensive everywhere — mortgages, car loans, business credit. When it lowers rates, borrowing becomes cheaper and expands. This single lever shapes the entire economy. Crucially, the Fed is not a government agency in the traditional sense — it is a quasi-private institution, and its decisions are made by a board that includes representatives from the major commercial banks it is supposed to regulate.

Each major country has its own central bank operating under similar principles and all of them coordinate, to varying degrees, through the BIS above them.

**Governments borrowing money: bond markets**
Governments do not fund themselves purely through taxes. They also borrow — and they do this by issuing **bonds**. A bond is essentially a government's written promise: "lend us $1,000 today and we will pay you back in 10 years, plus interest along the way." Investors — pension funds, foreign governments, banks, asset managers — buy these bonds.

The bond market is where this borrowing happens. And whoever controls it has enormous leverage over governments. If large bond holders decide a government's debt is too risky and stop buying its bonds, that government loses its ability to borrow. Interest rates on its debt spike. It faces a funding crisis. This is not theoretical — it has happened to Greece, Argentina, and others. Dixon's point is that this mechanism gives the financial sector a structural veto over government policy, without ever casting a vote.

**Where the money pools: asset managers**
While central banks create money and bond markets lend it to governments, **asset managers** are the firms that pool and deploy vast quantities of existing wealth. Companies like BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street manage money on behalf of pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, insurance companies, and wealthy individuals. Together they control tens of trillions of dollars in investable assets — meaning they are among the largest shareholders of nearly every major public company on earth simultaneously. Their investment decisions shape which industries grow, which companies survive, and which governments can access cheap capital.

**At the edges: the IMF and World Bank**
These are international institutions, nominally part of the United Nations system, that extend credit specifically to countries — usually developing nations in economic crisis. The **IMF** (International Monetary Fund) provides emergency loans when a country can't pay its debts. The **World Bank** funds longer-term development projects like infrastructure. Both come with conditions: policy changes the borrowing country must implement in exchange for the money. These conditions — which often include privatizing public services, cutting social spending, and opening markets to foreign capital — have been criticized for locking poor nations into permanent debt dependency rather than genuine development.

The critical insight Dixon returns to again and again: **all modern money is created as debt.** Here is what that actually means, step by step:

1. You go to a bank and ask for a $10,000 loan.
2. The bank does not go into a vault and retrieve $10,000 that someone else deposited. It types $10,000 into your account. That money did not exist before this moment. The bank created it.
3. You now owe $10,000 — plus interest. Say 8%. So you owe $10,800 total.
4. Here is the problem: the bank created the $10,000. But it did not create the $800 in interest. That $800 does not exist anywhere in the system yet.
5. To pay back the $800 in interest, you have to earn it — which means it has to come from money that exists somewhere else in the economy. Money that itself was created by another loan, charged with its own interest.

Multiply this across every mortgage, car loan, student loan, credit card, and government bond in the world, and you get a system where **the total debt always exceeds the total money supply**. There is never enough money in existence to pay back all the debt — because the interest was never created, only the principal.

This means the system has one requirement above all else: it must keep growing. New loans must constantly be issued to create the money needed to service old interest. Not because people are greedy (though some are), but because the math demands it. If lending stops, the system contracts. Companies default. Banks fail. Governments can't pay their obligations. The whole structure seizes.

So what forces new lending when it would otherwise slow down?

This is where Dixon draws the line between economics and geopolitics. A war requires a government to borrow enormous sums — to fund weapons procurement, troop deployment, reconstruction contracts, foreign aid. That borrowing creates a massive surge of new loans, which creates new money, which temporarily relieves the interest pressure built up across the whole system. A financial crisis does the same thing in reverse: banks fail, credit freezes, and governments step in with bailout programs — again funded by borrowing, again injecting new debt-money into the system. The 2008 bailout didn't pay off debt; it created trillions in new debt to paper over old debt.

Dixon's conclusion is that wars and crises are not interruptions to the financial system — they are, in his view, the system's way of generating the new borrowing it needs to survive. He does not require anyone to be consciously orchestrating this; institutional incentives are enough to produce the same outcome.

However, most economists and historians would draw the causal arrow differently. The mainstream view is that wars and financial crises have their own causes — political miscalculation, nationalism, ideological conflict, bank failures, asset bubbles — and that the borrowing that follows is a consequence, not a motive. The fact that debt expands during a war does not, in this view, mean the war happened in order to expand debt. Dixon's argument is a structural one about incentives, not a claim that anyone sat in a room and decided to start a war to generate lending — but critics argue the distinction matters, and that the pattern he identifies does not prove the mechanism he proposes.

But who actually decides which institutions get the new money — and which ones don't? That question points to one company more than any other.

**BlackRock** deserves special attention here. It manages roughly $10–14 trillion in assets and operates a risk-analysis platform called **Aladdin** that oversees an estimated $25 trillion across pension funds, central banks, and sovereign wealth funds. During the 2008 financial crisis and again during COVID, BlackRock was hired by the U.S. Treasury and Federal Reserve to manage bailout programs — meaning a private company was effectively making decisions about which institutions survived. Dixon argues this gives BlackRock extraordinary, largely invisible power over the global economy.

### The Military-Industrial Complex (MIC)

Unlike the FIC, this one has a name most people have heard — though usually without understanding what it actually means. In 1961, President Dwight Eisenhower, himself a five-star general who had commanded Allied forces in World War II, gave a farewell address warning the American public about what he called the "military-industrial complex." His concern: that the permanent defense industry created after World War II had become so large, so embedded in the economy, and so politically connected that it would come to shape foreign policy for its own benefit rather than the nation's.

That warning, Dixon argues, was not heeded. The MIC has only grown since.

**Defense contractors: the companies that profit from war**
A defense contractor is a private company whose primary customer is the government — specifically the military. Companies like Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, and Boeing design and manufacture weapons systems: fighter jets, missiles, drones, warships, surveillance equipment. They are publicly traded companies with shareholders who expect quarterly profits.

Here is the tension Dixon highlights: a weapons company's most profitable period is active conflict, not peace. In peacetime, governments might maintain existing equipment or develop new systems slowly. In wartime, procurement surges — contracts balloon, production ramps up, profits rise. Dixon argues this means the financial incentive structure of these companies is oriented toward conflict continuing rather than ending — that the profit motive and the peace motive are structurally opposed.

Others would note that having a financial incentive for something is not the same as causing it. Defense contractors lobby for contracts and budgets, but most analysts distinguish that institutional pressure from actually driving the decision to go to war, which involves heads of state, intelligence assessments, public opinion, and international law. The incentive is real; whether it determines outcomes is the contested part.

**The Pentagon and defense budgets**
The Pentagon is the headquarters of the U.S. Department of Defense and the nerve center of the largest military in history. The U.S. defense budget regularly exceeds $800 billion per year — more than the next ten countries combined. That money flows directly to defense contractors through procurement contracts. The relationship is intimate: senior Pentagon officials routinely move into executive roles at defense companies after retiring, and defense company lobbyists routinely shape the legislation that determines what gets funded. This revolving door is not incidental — it is how the MIC sustains itself.

**Intelligence agencies: the off-book layer**
The CIA, NSA, MI6, Mossad, and their equivalents operate in a space that is partially outside normal democratic accountability. Their budgets are classified. Their operations are not publicly disclosed. Their legal authorities are broad and often secret. Dixon's point is not that these agencies are uniquely evil — it is that they function as the MIC's deniable arm. They can destabilize governments, fund insurgencies, run influence operations, and gather intelligence on foreign leaders in ways that a uniformed military cannot. When a country needs to be pressured before a war, or after one, intelligence agencies are often the instrument.

**Think tanks: manufacturing the rationale**
Most people don't encounter think tanks directly, but they shape the policy environment that politicians operate in. A think tank is an organization — often funded by defense contractors, wealthy donors, or foreign governments — that produces research, reports, and expert opinion on foreign policy, security, and economics. Institutions like the Council on Foreign Relations, the RAND Corporation, the Atlantic Council, and the Brookings Institution publish the frameworks that journalists cite, that congressional staffers read, and that administration officials use to justify policy decisions.

Dixon's observation: the think tanks that receive the most defense industry funding tend to produce the most hawkish foreign policy recommendations. This is not conspiracy — it is basic institutional logic. Organizations produce outputs that serve their funders.

**The revenue logic of war**
Put it together and Dixon's argument comes into focus. A war is not just a military event — it is an economic one. Procurement contracts surge. The government borrows heavily to fund operations, injecting new debt-money into the system. Reconstruction contracts follow. Energy resources may be secured or disrupted in ways that benefit certain markets. Dixon argues that the narrative justifying intervention — the ideological or humanitarian case — is typically assembled and broadcast after the financial and strategic decisions have already been made. He puts this lag at roughly 18 months: by the time the public hears the argument for a war, the economic logic has, in his view, been in motion for a year and a half. This is his own observation, not a documented standard; it functions as a rule of thumb for how to read public events rather than a verified measurement.

The MIC depends entirely on the FIC — every defense contractor is a publicly traded company that needs access to capital markets, shareholders, and credit lines. The FIC funds the MIC. And the MIC, in turn, generates the instability that justifies the FIC's next round of crisis lending.

### The Technological-Industrial Complex (TIC)

The TIC is the newest of the three and, in Dixon's view, the fastest-growing. It is also the least visible — because its primary product is not a weapon or a financial instrument but something more intimate: the management of what people believe, what they pay attention to, and how they understand the world.

**Where it starts: DARPA**
DARPA stands for the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency. It is the U.S. military's research and development arm — funded by the defense budget, staffed by scientists and engineers, tasked with inventing technologies that give the military a strategic edge. What most people don't realize is how many technologies that now define everyday civilian life originated inside DARPA: the internet (originally ARPANET), GPS, the computer mouse, voice recognition, and foundational work in artificial intelligence all trace back to DARPA funding.

The pattern is consistent: the government funds the research, often for explicitly military purposes. Once the technology matures, it is transferred — through grants, contracts, or the careers of the researchers involved — into private companies that commercialize it. The public gets a product. The defense establishment retains access, often through contracts or classified arrangements that are not publicly disclosed. Silicon Valley, in this reading, is not a garage-startup story — it is a privatization story.

**Big Tech as the TIC's civilian face**
Google began with research funded partly through intelligence community grants. Palantir — a data analytics company that helps governments track populations and predict behavior — was founded with CIA seed funding through a venture arm called In-Q-Tel. Amazon Web Services runs classified cloud infrastructure for the CIA and NSA. Microsoft holds a major Pentagon contract. Meta's internal research has overlapping interests with behavioral influence programs studied by military psychologists.

None of this means these companies are simply government fronts. But Dixon's point is that the relationship between Silicon Valley and the intelligence and defense establishment is far closer than the consumer-facing brand identity of these companies suggests. They are not neutral platforms — they emerged from, and remain entangled with, the same institutional network as the MIC.

**Surveillance infrastructure**
Every smartphone carries a microphone, a camera, a GPS tracker, and a record of every communication its owner sends or receives. Every search query, purchase, and social media interaction is logged, stored, and analyzed. The infrastructure to monitor populations at scale — once the exclusive domain of authoritarian states — now exists globally, operated partly by private companies and partly by governments that access their data through legal demands, partnerships, or covert programs.

This infrastructure serves the FIC and MIC in a direct way: it allows the system to identify dissent early, track organizing, and build profiles of individuals who might become threats to institutional stability.

**Social media: the attention and division machine**
The platforms most people use daily — Facebook, X (formerly Twitter), YouTube, TikTok — are not primarily communication tools. They are attention capture systems whose revenue depends on maximizing the time users spend engaged. The most reliable way to keep people engaged, as every platform's internal research has confirmed, is to show them content that provokes strong emotional reactions: outrage, fear, tribal identity, moral indignation.

The result is a population that is perpetually agitated, sorted into opposing camps, and increasingly unable to agree on basic shared facts. Dixon's argument is that this is not an engineering accident — it is the output of a system working as designed, and that the division it produces conveniently prevents people from organizing around the structural questions the FIC and MIC would prefer remain invisible.

The alternative view is that social media companies are optimizing for engagement because engagement drives ad revenue — not because anyone planned to fracture society. The polarization is, in this reading, a side effect of a profit motive, not a feature of a control system. Both explanations point to the same observable harm; they differ on whether it is intentional. Dixon's framework treats the beneficiary of an outcome as evidence of its purpose, which critics argue is not the same thing.

## How the Three Complexes Relate

Described separately, the three complexes can sound like parallel systems. They are not — they are one system with three functions, each one enabling the others.

The FIC is the foundation. It controls who has money and on what terms. Every other institution in the world — governments, militaries, corporations, universities — needs access to capital to function. That dependency is the source of the FIC's power. It does not need to issue orders. It simply decides who gets funded and who doesn't.

The MIC is what the FIC deploys when financial leverage isn't enough. When a country resists the debt conditions the IMF attaches to its loans, or nationalizes its oil reserves, or trades outside the dollar system, the MIC provides the tools to destabilize it — through sanctions, covert operations, proxy conflicts, or direct military intervention. War is expensive, but it also generates the new borrowing the FIC needs to keep the debt system expanding. The MIC and FIC are not in tension — they are mutually reinforcing.

The TIC is what keeps populations from seeing or acting on any of this. A population that understands how money is created, who controls the bond markets, and why wars follow financial incentives is a population that might organize to change things. A population that is sorted into warring political tribes, addicted to outrage, and surveilled closely enough that dissent can be identified early — that population is manageable. The TIC does not need to be overtly repressive. It just needs to keep attention pointed at itself.

**Where governments fit**
Dixon's most provocative claim may be the simplest one: governments are not at the top of this structure. They are inside it.

A newly elected president or prime minister inherits a country already deep in debt — which means already obligated to the bond markets and the FIC's lending conditions. They inherit a military with its own institutional interests, its own contractor relationships, its own intelligence apparatus that predates their administration and will outlast it. They inherit a media and technology environment they did not build and largely cannot control. The space for genuine independent decision-making is narrow.

Dixon describes politicians as "well-paid narrators." In his view, their job is not to run the system — it is to make the system's outputs legible and acceptable to the public. They provide the face, the speech, the moral framing, while the structural decisions are made by institutions that don't appear on any ballot.

The opposing view is that elected governments retain genuine, if constrained, power — and that the degree of constraint varies significantly depending on the country, the leader, and the moment. Critics of Dixon's framing argue it leaves no room for political agency, which can lead to fatalism: if governments are just actors, there is no point in democratic participation. Dixon's supporters would respond that acknowledging constraints is not the same as saying nothing matters — it is a precondition for understanding where pressure is actually worth applying.

## Currency: Why the Dollar Rules, and What That Means

The mechanics of money creation were covered in the FIC section. But there is another layer of the currency question Dixon returns to repeatedly: not just how money is created, but why the U.S. dollar specifically sits at the center of the entire global system — and what that position allows.

**The petrodollar system**
After World War II, the major Western powers agreed that global trade would be conducted in U.S. dollars, and that the dollar would be backed by gold at a fixed rate. This gave the U.S. enormous advantages: other countries had to acquire dollars to participate in international trade, which created permanent global demand for the currency.

In 1971, President Nixon broke the link between the dollar and gold entirely. The dollar was no longer backed by anything tangible — it was "fiat," meaning it had value because the U.S. government declared it legal tender and because the rest of the world had no practical alternative. To maintain demand for the dollar after the gold link was severed, the U.S. struck a deal with Saudi Arabia: oil — the most essential commodity in the global economy — would be priced and sold exclusively in dollars. Any country that wanted to buy oil had to first acquire dollars. This is the **petrodollar system**, and it has underwritten dollar dominance ever since.

The consequence: the U.S. can print dollars and spend them globally in a way no other country can. Other nations must earn dollars through exports or borrow them. The U.S. simply creates them. This is sometimes called "exorbitant privilege" — and it is one of the primary mechanisms through which the FIC maintains American financial dominance.

**What happens when countries try to exit**
Dixon connects the petrodollar directly to military intervention. He points to a pattern: several countries that moved to trade oil outside the dollar system — Iraq under Saddam Hussein, Libya under Gaddafi, Iran — faced invasion or severe destabilization in the years that followed. Dixon treats this pattern as structural evidence that threatening dollar dominance triggers a response from the MIC.

However, each of those interventions had its own stated justifications — weapons of mass destruction in Iraq, humanitarian crisis in Libya, nuclear ambitions in Iran — and mainstream foreign policy analysts argue those factors were the primary drivers. The currency challenge may have been one consideration among many, or coincidental to the timing entirely. Dixon's argument requires accepting that the publicly stated reasons were pretexts, which is itself an interpretive position rather than an established fact.

**Who benefits from inflation**
When new money is created — through loans, through government spending, through central bank asset purchases — it enters the economy at specific points first. Banks get it. Large financial institutions get it. Asset prices rise before consumer prices do. By the time inflation shows up in grocery stores and rent, the people closest to the money creation have already deployed it into appreciating assets. The person who owns property, stocks, and bonds sees their wealth inflate alongside the money supply. The person whose only asset is their paycheck watches their purchasing power erode. Dixon calls this the structural advantage built into the system for those at the top — it does not require corruption or conspiracy, only proximity to where money enters the economy.

## Bitcoin: The Exit, Not the Reform

After mapping the system in full, Dixon's conclusion is not that it can be fixed. His view is that reform efforts are absorbed and neutralized — that the system is self-sealing. Regulatory bodies get captured by the industries they regulate. Political candidates who challenge the FIC find their funding dries up or their careers end. Movements that threaten institutional power tend to get infiltrated, co-opted, or reframed into something harmless.

His response to this is not despair — it is a different kind of move: exit rather than reform.

**Why most alternatives don't work**
Gold is the historical alternative to fiat currency — a physical asset that no government can print more of. Dixon acknowledges its appeal but notes its limits: gold can be seized. In 1933, the U.S. government made it illegal for citizens to privately own gold and forced them to exchange it for dollars. A government that controls territory and law enforcement can confiscate physical assets. This is not a theoretical risk — it has happened.

There is also a generational dimension worth noting here. Cathie Wood, CEO of ARK Invest — one of the largest and most prominent Bitcoin investors in traditional finance — has argued that younger generations are increasingly treating Bitcoin as their version of gold: a hard asset outside the financial system that holds value over time. Her research suggests this shift is already underway and will accelerate as wealth transfers from older generations who trusted gold to younger ones who grew up digital. This framing — Bitcoin as digital gold — is widely shared among long-term Bitcoin investors and is broadly consistent with Dixon's view of why gold is losing its role. Where Dixon focuses on gold's seizability as the structural flaw, Wood focuses on its portability and accessibility problems. Both arrive at Bitcoin as the superior alternative, though for somewhat different reasons.

Other financial assets — stocks, bonds, real estate — exist entirely within the FIC's infrastructure. They are denominated in fiat currency, held through institutions that operate under government licenses, and subject to capital controls, freezes, and confiscation orders. Owning these things means participating in the system, on the system's terms.

**What Bitcoin is, in plain terms**
Bitcoin is digital money that runs on a network with no central authority — no company that owns it, no government that issues it, no bank that holds it. It was released in 2009 by a person or group using the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto, whose real identity remains unknown.

Three properties make it significant in Dixon's framework:

The supply is fixed at 21 million coins, hardcoded into the software. No institution can create more — not a central bank, not a government, not even the original developers. This makes it the first form of money in history that is structurally resistant to inflation through issuance.

It is decentralized. Thousands of computers around the world each hold a full copy of the transaction record. To alter or shut down the network, you would need to simultaneously overpower the majority of those computers — a task no single government or institution has managed.

It is self-custodial. If you hold Bitcoin in your own wallet — meaning you hold the private cryptographic key yourself, not through an exchange or a bank — no institution can freeze it, seize it, or deny you access. It exists as mathematics, not as an entry in someone else's ledger.

**Dixon's theory about Bitcoin's origins**
Dixon offers a provocative theory about where Bitcoin came from. In his reading, it was originally conceived as an intelligence project — an attempt to design a controllable digital currency that could eventually replace cash and give governments total visibility into every transaction. The anonymous "Satoshi Nakamoto" may have been a team rather than an individual, possibly with intelligence community ties. Dixon believes that **Len Sassaman** — a prominent cryptographer and privacy activist who died in 2011 — may have been involved in either creating Bitcoin or ensuring it was released in a form that couldn't be controlled. By making the code fully open-source and the cryptographic architecture genuinely resistant to seizure, whoever built it created something the system has spent years trying to recapture — through regulation, institutional products that bring Bitcoin back inside the financial system, and the development of Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) that mimic Bitcoin's technology while restoring central control.

This is speculation, not established history. There is no confirmed evidence linking Bitcoin's origins to any intelligence agency, and Len Sassaman's possible involvement is one of several Satoshi theories that circulate in the Bitcoin community — none of which has been proven. The majority view among cryptographers and Bitcoin researchers is that Satoshi Nakamoto was an individual or small group motivated by libertarian and cypherpunk ideals, not a state project that escaped its handlers. Dixon's theory is worth knowing because it shapes his entire framework — but it should be held as a hypothesis, not a conclusion.

**The recapture in progress: Bitcoin ETFs**
The most visible example of the system attempting to recapture Bitcoin is the Bitcoin ETF. An ETF — Exchange-Traded Fund — is a financial product that trades on a stock exchange and tracks the price of an underlying asset. You don't own the asset itself; you own a share of a fund that owns it on your behalf. ETFs exist for oil, gold, real estate, and now Bitcoin. In 2024, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission approved the first spot Bitcoin ETFs, and institutions rushed in. BlackRock's iShares Bitcoin ETF became one of the fastest-growing ETFs in history, accumulating hundreds of billions in assets within its first year.

From a mainstream investment perspective, this looks like validation — Bitcoin going legitimate, institutional money flowing in, the asset maturing. Cathie Wood of ARK Invest, whose firm also runs a Bitcoin ETF, sees institutional adoption of this kind as one of the primary drivers of Bitcoin's long-term price appreciation. She has projected Bitcoin reaching between $750,000 and $1.2 million by 2030, with institutional allocation being a core part of the thesis.

Dixon's view of the same events is almost the inverse. When you buy a Bitcoin ETF, you do not hold Bitcoin — BlackRock does. The self-custodial property that Dixon identifies as Bitcoin's defining feature — the fact that no institution can freeze or seize it — disappears the moment it moves into an ETF. The Bitcoin is now inside the banking system, held by the same asset manager that managed the government's crisis bailout programs. Dixon would argue that institutional adoption through ETFs is not Bitcoin winning. It is the FIC finding a way to hold the keys.

**What Dixon recommends**
His practical advice follows directly from the analysis. If the system extracts wealth through debt, reduce your debt. If it controls assets through financial institutions, hold some assets outside those institutions. If it monitors and controls through centralized platforms, diversify across jurisdictions. And if you want a form of money that exists outside the FIC's reach, Bitcoin — held in self-custody — is currently the only option he identifies as structurally adequate.

The goal is not to overthrow the system. It is to reduce your personal dependency on it, so that its crises become less your problem.

## The Core Claim, Simply Put

The world is not run by governments elected by citizens. It is run by the incentive structures of three interlocking complexes — financial, military, and technological — that predate most living governments and will outlast most current ones. Politicians operate within these structures. Wars follow financial logic, not ideology. Currency is a control mechanism. Technology manages perception. And the first step toward freedom, in Dixon's view, is simply seeing the map.
